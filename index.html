I am an Associate Professor and Chair of Economics at UC Merced, where I study labor markets, immigration, and the economics of innovation. My research asks how workers and firms adapt to large structural forces — trade liberalization, technological change, immigration policy, and knowledge spillovers — with particular attention to the margins that standard datasets miss.

I received my Ph.D. in Economics from UC Davis and B.A. from UC Berkeley.

The Missing Margin: Internal Mobility and the Measurement of Monopsony Power
Greg C. Wright  ·  2024

Standard measures of employer monopsony power use job separations to infer worker outside options, but ignore within-firm mobility. This paper shows that H-1B visa restrictions create a sharp, policy-driven wedge between internal and external mobility for affected workers. Exploiting this variation, I find that conventional monopsony estimates are substantially upward-biased: much of the apparent wage markdown reflects internal reallocation that standard frameworks misclassify as captive labor.

Immigration, Offshoring and American Jobs
With Giovanni Peri and Gianmarco Ottaviano  ·  American Economic Review, August 2013, Vol. 103 No. 5

Immigration and offshoring both shift labor supply in U.S. markets, but through different channels. We show that immigration tends to complement native employment by filling task-content gaps, while offshoring displaces routine-intensive work. Jointly accounting for both forces reconciles earlier conflicting findings in the immigration and trade literatures.

Revisiting the Employment Impact of Offshoring
Greg C. Wright  ·  European Economic Review, February 2014, Vol. 66

Re-examining the employment effects of offshore production using improved industry-level data and an instrumental variables strategy, I find that the negative employment effects of offshoring are concentrated in manufacturing industries with high routine task content and are substantially larger than earlier estimates suggested.

A Short-Run View of What Computers Do: Evidence from a U.K. Tax Incentive
With Paul Gaggl  ·  American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, July 2017, Vol. 9 No. 3

Exploiting a temporary U.K. tax incentive for IT investment as a source of exogenous variation in computerization, we trace the short-run effects on occupation structure within firms. IT adoption rapidly shifts labor demand toward communication-intensive tasks and away from routine clerical work, consistent with a task-based model of technology adoption.

From Selling Goods to Selling Services: Firm Responses to Trade Liberalization
With Holger Breinlich and Anson Soderbery  ·  American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, November 2018, Vol. 10 No. 4

Using detailed U.K. firm-product data and tariff variation from EU trade agreements, we show that trade liberalization causes firms to rebalance their revenue mix away from goods and toward services. This margin of adjustment — largely invisible to trade statistics — accounts for a substantial share of the aggregate employment response to import competition.

Immigration, Trade and Productivity in Services: Evidence from U.K. Firms
With Giovanni Peri and Gianmarco Ottaviano  ·  Journal of International Economics, May 2018, Vol. 112

Combining employer-employee matched data with firm-level trade records, we show that immigration raises firm-level productivity in services primarily by expanding the set of trading partners. Firms that hire immigrants from a given country are significantly more likely to subsequently export to and import from that country.

Processing Immigration Shocks: Firm Responses on the Innovation Margin
With Rowena Gray and Giulia Montresor  ·  Journal of International Economics, September 2020, Vol. 126

We show that immigration-driven increases in high-skilled labor supply raise firm patenting activity, particularly in technology classes where immigrants have comparative advantage. The innovation response is faster and larger at firms with prior experience employing foreign workers, suggesting that absorptive capacity shapes the returns to immigrant talent.

The Human Capital Legacy of a Trade Embargo
With Matthias Parey and Abhishek Chakravarty  ·  Journal of the European Economic Association, June 2021

We use the U.S. embargo on Cuba as a natural experiment to identify the long-run effects of trade disruption on human capital accumulation. Cohorts that came of age during the embargo show persistently lower educational attainment and occupational complexity, with effects concentrated among those who would otherwise have worked in export-oriented sectors.

A Rising Tide? The Local Incidence of the Second Wave of Globalization
With Rowena Gray  ·  Journal of International Economics, March 2024, Vol. 148

We examine the distributional effects of the post-2000 expansion of global value chains on U.S. local labor markets, finding that the gains from the second wave of globalization were concentrated among higher-skilled workers and regions with greater initial exposure to manufacturing, widening inequality within already-diverging places.
